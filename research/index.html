Electronic and magnetic properties of matter

My research focuses on the electronic structure and properties of complex quantum materials ranging from semiconductors, topological insulators, unconventional magnets to low-dimensional van der Waals crystals, with particular interest in the non-equilibrium behaviours derived from the many-body interactions that underpin their physical properties. My aim is to understand the fundamental nature of the exotic phases in these materials, and manipulate their electronic and collective phenomena at the ultrafast timescale in view of applications in the fabrication of devices with improved performances. My activity is carried out mostly in large-scale user facilities, which I consider my favourite playground: I performed experiments in several synchrotron radiation facilities such as Elettra (IT), Diamond Light Source (UK), ESRF and SOLEIL (FR). Moreover, I work in tabletop laser laboratories, having access to state-of-the-art instrumentation for femtosecond-resolved spectroscopies in a pump-probe configuration.

The discovery of graphene in 2004 has kickstarted a considerable amount of research into two-dimensional (2D) systems. Little was in fact known in practice about the behaviour of a material when confined into a purely 2D state, i.e. a single atomic layer. A lot of theoretical efforts have been diverted towards the study of these systems, and one of the most well-known results is the Mermin-Wagner theorem. "Continuous symmetries cannot be spontaneously broken in a system with sufficiently short-range interactions if its dimensionality is <=2." One application of this theorem has to do with long-range magnetic order: the continuous symmetry is the spin orientation, the short-range interaction is exchange, and the dimensionality is the real-space extension of the crystal. In terms of magnetism, the Mermin-Wagner theorem reads "Long-range magnetic order cannot be realized in 2D systems".
